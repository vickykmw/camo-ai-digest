However, predicting their precise impact a priori is challenging, given AI's simultaneous effects on both demand and supply, as well as the strategic responses of market participants. **Summary (Claude):**

> When LLMs are used as stand-ins for human participants in behavioural economics experiments, do they actually replicate human decision-making? This study tests that assumption across two classic games — a p-beauty contest and a public goods game — varying model scale, temperature, quantisation, and instruction-tuning across 360 conditions. The finding is that LLMs behave as fixed, scale-determined 'level-k' reasoners: larger models reason more steps ahead, but none update beliefs dynamically across rounds or backward-induct from a game's end. Human contributions to public goods decay over rounds; LLMs stay flat or rise. LLMs respond to horizon framing (indefinite vs finite) but ignore their actual position within that horizon, so no end-game defection appears. The implication is that LLMs simulate a cognitive category, not a cognitive process.

**CAMO angle:**

> This study argues that LLMs, when used as synthetic participants in behavioural games, recover surface-level choice distributions but not the underlying reasoning process — they retrieve a strategic level rather than computing one. From CAMO's perspective on algorithmic foundations for trustworthy AI-driven decisions, this is a calibration problem with practical consequence: any organisation or researcher using LLM-simulated agents to pilot-test incentive schemes, mechanism designs, or market experiments should treat the outputs as category-level approximations, not behavioural ground truth. The gap between simulated cooperation and real end-game defection, for instance, would systematically mislead predictions about how employees or counterparties behave when stakes and horizons become concrete.

**Matched CAMO research:**

**Key takeaway:** LLMs mimic a fixed level of strategic reasoning set by model scale but do not update beliefs or backward-induct, making them unreliable substitutes for human participants in dynamic behavioural experiments.

**Summary (Claude):**

> When players in Baldur's Gate 3 notice that AI-driven game mechanics are designed to deceive them, their satisfaction ratings fall—regardless of how intense that deception actually is. Using six years of Steam reviews and 54 version updates as a quasi-natural experiment, this study separates designer-intended deception intensity from players' perceived deception awareness. The key finding: any degree of deception awareness produces a monotonic negative effect on positive review rates, falsifying the common assumption that moderate perceived deception is tolerable or even enjoyable. High-intensity deception updates appear positive only because they coincide with new content releases—not because players appreciate the deception itself. Player experience does not reliably moderate these effects.

**CAMO angle:**

> This study demonstrates that perceived AI deception—distinct from designed deception intensity—exerts a consistent, negative causal effect on user evaluations in a high-engagement digital environment. From CAMO's perspective on the algorithmic and data foundations of trustworthy AI systems, the finding sharpens a measurement problem that matters well beyond gaming: designers and deployers may believe they have calibrated an AI behavior at an acceptable intensity, while users are perceiving something altogether different. The gap between intended system design and user attribution is precisely the kind of confound that rigorous causal identification—of the type explored in CAMO's work on algorithmic decision-making and endogenous data—needs to account for before drawing policy or product conclusions.
