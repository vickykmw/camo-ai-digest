### 4. Are Rationales Necessary and Sufficient? Tuning LLMs for Explainable Misinformation Detection

- [ ] **APPROVE FOR SOCIAL** (pillar: `AI Algorithms & Data`)

- **Source:** arXiv: Computers & Society (cs.CY)
- **Link:** <https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.19285>
- **Published:** May 20, 2026
- **Author(s):** Bing Wang, Rui Miao, Ximing Li, Chen Shen, Shaotian Yan, Changchun Li, Kaiyuan Liu, Xiaosong Yuan, Jieping Ye
- **Keyword score:** 8.94 (AI strong: 7, AI booster: 0, domain: 2)
- **Pillar:** AI Algorithms & Data  ·  **Pillar relevance:** 72/100
- **Audience fit:** managers/C-suite: low · policy makers: medium · general public: low

**Summary (Claude):**

> When large language models explain why a piece of content is misinformation, those explanations can be either too thin to justify the verdict or bloated with unnecessary verification steps. This paper identifies both failure modes in standard fine-tuning pipelines for misinformation detection and proposes LONSREX, a data synthesis method that scores each reasoning step by how much it actually contributes to the final prediction. By keeping only reasoning steps that are both necessary and sufficient, the resulting model produces explanations that are tighter, more accurate, and better calibrated to the evidence. Experiments confirm the approach outperforms naive label-filtered baselines. The work addresses a practical bottleneck: deploying LLMs as credible fact-checking tools requires not just correct verdicts but defensible reasoning chains.

**CAMO angle:**

> This paper argues that explainable AI for misinformation detection fails not because models reach wrong conclusions, but because the reasoning they generate is either under-supported or unnecessarily verbose—and that filtering training data on label correctness alone does not solve either problem. From CAMO's perspective on algorithmic trustworthiness, this is a concrete illustration of why accuracy metrics are insufficient proxies for decision quality: the reasoning chain that accompanies a prediction carries its own validity requirements, and organizations deploying AI for content moderation or compliance will inherit liability for that reasoning, not just for the binary output. The LONSREX contribution—quantifying each verification step's marginal contribution—offers a design principle applicable well beyond misinformation, wherever AI-generated rationales are used to justify consequential decisions.

**Matched CAMO research:**

- [AI Persuasion, Bayesian Attribution, and Career Concern of Doctors](https://camo.hku.hk/ai-persuasion-bayesian-attribution-and-career-concern-of-doctors/) — Both papers examine how the quality and interpretability of AI-generated reasoning shapes whether human decision-makers can meaningfully evaluate and act on an AI's output—one in medical diagnosis, the other in fact-checking.

**Key takeaway:** Getting an AI to flag misinformation correctly is only half the problem; the reasoning it offers must also be traceable and proportionate, or the system cannot be trusted in practice.
