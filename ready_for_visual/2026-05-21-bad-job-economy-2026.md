# Content Cluster — Toward a Bad Job Economy: AI Adoption, Agency Costs, and Job Design

_Created: 2026-05-21  ·  Cluster id: `bad-job-economy-2026`  ·  3 articles_

## Anchor CAMO research

**[Toward a Bad Job Economy: AI Adoption, Agency Costs, and Job Design](https://camo.hku.hk/toward-a-bad-job-economy-ai-adoption-agency-costs-and-job-design/)**
_Matthias Fahn, Jin Li, Chang Sun  ·  2026  ·  Working Paper_

**Pillars:** AI & Incentives, AI Adoption, AI & Jobs

**Abstract:**

> We study how AI affects compensation and job design when performance depends on workers' non-contractible effort. In a principal–agent model with limited liability, AI reduces effort costs but disproportionately lowers the cost of achieving satisfactory performance. This raises the incentive cost of sustaining high effort and can induce firms to replace high-wage, high-effort good jobs with low-wage, low-effort bad jobs, even when good jobs create more total surplus. As a result, AI can lower wages, reduce worker welfare, and even depress profits. If workers can adopt AI unilaterally, adoption occurs even when the resulting equilibrium harms both parties; when adoption requires worker cooperation, resistance is strongest where AI erodes rents embodied in good jobs. In a search-and-matching extension, endogenous outside options amplify these forces, reinforcing a bad-job economy and potentially reducing employment.

### 1. Beyond the Model — Why Responsible AI Must Address Workforce Impact

- **Source:** MIT Sloan Management Review
- **Link:** <https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/beyond-the-model-why-responsible-ai-must-address-workforce-impact/>
- **Published:** Apr 21, 2026
- **Pillar:** AI & Jobs

**Summary:**

> A fifth consecutive annual survey by MIT Sloan Management Review and Boston Consulting Group examines how organizations are implementing responsible AI (RAI) worldwide. This year's edition shifts attention toward workforce impact as an underexamined dimension of RAI — arguing that responsible AI frameworks have focused heavily on model-level concerns (bias, explainability, safety) while neglecting the organizational and human consequences of deployment: job displacement, skill degradation, shifting accountability, and uneven distribution of AI's benefits and burdens. The article draws on an international panel of academics and practitioners to assess RAI maturity and calls on organizations to extend their governance frameworks to cover how AI reshapes work itself. Note: the full findings are behind a paywall; this summary is based on the RSS excerpt and title framing.

**CAMO angle:**

> This article argues that responsible AI governance has a blind spot: it evaluates models but not the workforce consequences of deploying them. From CAMO's perspective on AI and jobs, this gap is well-evidenced in practice — our research on the hidden competency penalty for AI users (see 'Research: The Hidden Penalty of Using AI at Work') shows that even when organizations provide AI access, the social and evaluative dynamics around its use can harm the very workers it is meant to help. Extending RAI frameworks to include job design, accountability attribution, and adoption incentives is not peripheral to responsible AI — it is central to whether deployment produces net benefit for organizations and the people inside them.

**Key takeaway:** Responsible AI frameworks that stop at the model layer — ignoring how deployment reshapes jobs, accountability, and worker welfare — are incomplete governance, not just incomplete ethics.

### 2. AI, strategy, and the future of work: Oxford economist Jean-Paul Carvalho

- **Source:** McKinsey Insights
- **Link:** <https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/ai-strategy-and-the-future-of-work-oxford-economist-jean-paul-carvalho>
- **Published:** May 06, 2026
- **Pillar:** AI Adoption

**Summary:**

> Oxford economist Jean-Paul Carvalho, speaking with McKinsey, examines how AI is restructuring cognitive work and what that means for organizational strategy and business value creation. The excerpt is limited, but the framing centers on where AI creates leverage in knowledge-intensive tasks, how firms should position themselves to capture that value at scale, and what the redistribution of cognitive labor implies for workforce design. Because only the RSS excerpt is available, specific empirical findings cannot be confirmed; the conversation appears to be analytical and conceptual rather than data-driven.

**CAMO angle:**

> This McKinsey interview raises the question of where AI-driven productivity in cognitive work actually accrues — to firms, workers, or neither. From CAMO's perspective on AI adoption, the organizational challenge is not simply deploying AI but redesigning roles and incentive structures so that productivity gains translate into durable value rather than dissipating through coordination failures or eroded effort. CAMO's work on job design — including findings that AI can systematically shift firms toward lower-effort, lower-wage equilibria even when higher-effort configurations create more total surplus — suggests that 'capturing value at scale' depends as much on compensation architecture as on technical capability.

**Key takeaway:** Capturing value from AI in cognitive work is primarily an organizational and incentive design challenge, not a technical one.

### 3. The AI assembly line: Strategic imperatives for CEOs

- **Source:** McKinsey Insights
- **Link:** <https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/industrials/our-insights/the-ai-assembly-line-strategic-imperatives-for-ceos>
- **Published:** May 13, 2026
- **Pillar:** AI Adoption

**Summary:**

> McKinsey argues that AI's value creation potential has been demonstrated but remains difficult to scale across organizations. The central challenge for CEOs is moving from isolated experimentation to sustained, enterprise-wide performance improvement. The article frames this as a strategic and organizational design problem rather than a technical one: firms must reimagine structures, workflows, and operating models to capture AI's benefits systematically. The excerpt is thin on specific findings or data, so the precise mechanisms McKinsey recommends are not fully visible from the RSS summary alone. The overall argument is that AI deployment is now a CEO-level organizational transformation challenge, not a pilot program to be managed at the margin.

**CAMO angle:**

> This McKinsey piece argues that scaling AI requires CEOs to redesign organizational structures, not simply expand technical pilots. From CAMO's perspective on AI adoption, the harder problem is not access to AI tools but the organizational and incentive frictions that prevent genuine use at scale — a pattern documented in our Deloitte-HKU AI Adoption Index, where nearly half of executives reported initiatives fell short of expected ROI, with siloed departments and cultural resistance as the leading culprits. The implication is that CEO attention to org-level redesign is necessary but insufficient without deliberate attention to how AI changes job boundaries, accountability, and the conditions under which workers actually adopt these tools.

**Key takeaway:** Scaling AI across an organization is primarily an organizational design problem, and CEOs who treat it as a technical rollout are likely to leave most of the value on the table.
